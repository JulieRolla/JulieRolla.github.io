What Is Nebulous?

Nebulous is a research collaboration focused on inventing and optimizing new physical hardware architectures for science applications. It uses AI-driven design methods to create optimized, buildable detector technologies and scientific hardware — including spacecraft, antennas, and emerging instrument concepts — that must operate under tight mass, volume, and performance constraints.

At the heart of Nebulous is a dedicated software framework called ECLIPSE (Evolutionary Computation Library for Instrument Prototyping in Scientific Engineering). ECLIPSE is an evolutionary computation and physics-aware design library that searches vast, unconventional hardware design spaces. It encodes candidate designs as modular geometries, evaluates them with physics-based models, and iteratively improves them to meet science and engineering requirements.

Together, Nebulous and ECLIPSE blend computational creativity with engineering, physics, and computer science. The collaboration focuses on real, buildable hardware: designs must satisfy mission constraints, manufacturability, and performance targets, not just look good in simulation.

Current Focus Areas

Nebulous currently applies the ECLIPSE framework to several concrete engineering problems. Our work today is centered on three main areas:

  • AI-designed antennas and antenna arrays. Using ECLIPSE, we design antennas and interferometric arrays from scratch with evolutionary algorithms and modular, “LEGO-like” building blocks. The library explores many possible geometries and layouts, converging on high-performing, buildable solutions that meet mission-specific performance and footprint constraints.
  • Spacecraft geometries in LEO and VLEO. ECLIPSE is used to optimize spacecraft shapes and configurations for low Earth orbit (LEO), very low Earth orbit (VLEO), and high-variability drag environments. The framework trades off drag, power, pointing, and payload volume, helping mission teams understand how geometry impacts performance and lifetime.
  • Early-stage deployability with origami-inspired folds. Nebulous is in early R&D on using ECLIPSE to study deployable structures, applying origami-inspired folding patterns to improve how large surfaces and instruments can compactly stow and reliably deploy in space.

These are the areas where Nebulous and ECLIPSE are actively in use today. Additional applications — including other classes of detectors, ground-based instruments, and advanced structures — are explicitly treated as future directions under development, not as completed capabilities.

Future Applications

Looking ahead, the same framework that designs antennas, arrays, and spacecraft can be extended to a broader family of scientific hardware. We are exploring concepts for applying ECLIPSE to new detector technologies, ground-based instruments, and multi-physics structures where geometry, materials, and deployment all interact.

These future applications are actively being studied and developed, but they are intentionally described as emerging directions rather than completed capabilities. As partnerships and science cases grow, Nebulous and ECLIPSE will expand in step with real mission and instrument needs.

Why Nebulous?

Modern science missions and instruments must satisfy demanding performance requirements under tight mass, volume, schedule, and budget constraints. In traditional workflows, teams often cycle through many rounds of manual design and redesign before they reach a configuration that meets those requirements.

Nebulous was created to help science and engineering teams move more quickly toward viable designs. By using ECLIPSE to explore large design spaces computationally — and by embedding physics-based evaluation directly into that exploration — the collaboration aims to surface promising architectures earlier in the process. This can support designs that meet key science and engineering requirements in earlier generations of a project, reduce the need for repeated large-scale redesigns, and help teams use their resources more effectively.

The goal is not to replace human designers, but to give them a powerful partner that accelerates convergence to science-ready, buildable solutions.

Mission, Vision & Brand Essence

Mission

Nebulous develops AI-guided, physics-informed design tools that turn vast, constrained design spaces into buildable, high-performance hardware solutions for spacecraft, antennas, instruments, and advanced engineering systems.

Vision

Nebulous envisions a future where the hardest engineering problems are no longer limited by intuition alone, but expanded by intelligent search—revealing new designs that accelerate discovery, strengthen space exploration, and enable breakthrough technologies on Earth.

Slogan: Where uncertainty becomes innovation.

Brand Essence

The name “Nebulous” reflects the project’s commitment to exploration and emergence: ideas and structures forming from apparent ambiguity. The brand is grounded in four themes:

  • Exploration & Emergence: Structures and concepts emerging from complexity, directly tied to the cosmic imagery of a nebula.
  • Precision & Engineering: Every design is rooted in rigorous physics, optimization, and real-world buildability.
  • Scalability & Adaptability: A flexible system that can expand across different missions, domains, and generations of technology.
  • Interdisciplinary Reach: A framework that resonates with and serves NASA programs, defense partners, commercial industry, and academic collaborators.

In essence, Nebulous is about inventing the possible: designing the instruments and spacecraft of the future through intelligent, evolutionary exploration.

Founder & Collaboration

Nebulous was founded by Dr. Julie Rolla, a research scientist at NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory with a PhD in astroparticle physics. She leads the Nebulous collaboration and the development of ECLIPSE, focusing on AI-driven optimization of detector technologies and scientific hardware. Her work spans astrophysics, instrument design, and AI-driven optimization, as well as Earth science and the application of remote sensing products for disaster response.

The project brings together collaborators across NASA centers and universities, including NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, Michigan State University, The Ohio State University, the University of Colorado Boulder, McMaster University, Autogenetics Research Lab, and Remcom. This growing network of scientists, engineers, technologists, and students helps ensure that Nebulous is shaped by real science drivers and operational constraints.

By connecting evolutionary design, physics-based modeling, and mission needs, Nebulous aims to become a trusted partner for teams tackling their most challenging hardware design problems.

Who Nebulous Is For

Nebulous is designed for teams that need to explore ambitious hardware concepts under real-world constraints. It brings evolutionary design methods into early-stage hardware development, helping teams search larger design spaces without losing sight of requirements, risks, performance, and buildability.

  • Space agencies and mission formulation teams seeking better ways to explore trades between geometry, performance, and risk.
  • Commercial and defense partners developing compact, high-performance antennas, spacecraft, or deployable structures.
  • Academic researchers and students interested in the intersection of AI, physics, and hardware design.

Whether you are defining a new mission concept, rethinking an existing instrument, or exploring entirely new architectures, Nebulous can provide a framework for turning uncertainty into innovation.
